\begin{abstract}

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) and diabetic macular edema (DME) are among the leading causes of preventable vision loss in people with diabetes. Regular retinal screening is critical for early detection, yet access to trained eye care specialists remains limited in many parts of the world. Automated analysis of fundus photographs offers a practical way to support screening at scale. However, most existing deep learning systems treat DR and DME as two independent problems, each requiring its own model. This separation overlooks the fact that both conditions share underlying pathological features visible in the same retinal photograph, and it adds unnecessary computational overhead when both assessments are clinically required together.

In this work, we propose DR-ASPP-DRN, a multi-task deep learning framework that grades DR severity and estimates DME risk simultaneously from a single fundus image. The architecture combines a dilated ResNet50 backbone with an Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) module. The key idea is that by modifying one striding parameter in the backbone, the spatial resolution of the extracted feature maps is doubled, allowing the network to better represent small lesions such as microaneurysms that are diagnostically relevant but easily lost under standard resolution settings. The ASPP module then aggregates features across multiple receptive field scales, capturing retinal abnormalities that range from tiny dot haemorrhages to larger ischaemic regions. Two task-specific classification heads are trained jointly to predict five DR severity grades and three DME risk levels from a shared feature representation.

We evaluate the model on the IDRiD dataset. On the internal validation set, the model achieves quadratic weighted kappa (QWK) scores of 0.843 for DR grading and 0.876 for DME grading. These results are produced by a single forward pass through a 170 MB model and are consistent across two independent training runs, suggesting the training procedure converges reliably. We also evaluate the model on three external datasets without any additional fine-tuning: the IDRiD official test set (DR QWK = 0.580), Messidor (DR QWK = 0.436), and APTOS 2019 (DR QWK up to 0.563). The external results reflect the expected limitations of a model trained on a small single-institution dataset when applied to different populations and imaging conditions, and we discuss these limitations openly.

Beyond the reported results, the paper documents the training challenges that arise when working with a dataset of around 330 images, including the majority-class collapse behaviour observed in ten standard-configuration ablation variants, and the specific design choices required to overcome it. We hope both the architecture and the training methodology are useful reference points for similar small-dataset medical imaging problems.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Diabetic Retinopathy, Diabetic Macular Edema, Multi-Task Learning, Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling, Dilated ResNet, Quadratic Weighted Kappa

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

\subsection{The Scale of the Problem}

Diabetes is one of the more pressing public health challenges of this century. By the end of 2021, roughly 537 million adults were living with the disease worldwide, and the International Diabetes Federation projects this number will climb to around 783 million by 2045~\cite{IDF2021}. For most people, diabetes is understood mainly as a problem of blood sugar regulation. What receives less attention outside clinical settings is how consistently uncontrolled diabetes damages the small blood vessels of various organs. Among the secondary complications, ocular disease carries a particularly high burden, and much of it is preventable.

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is the most common microvascular complication of diabetes and a leading cause of new cases of legal blindness among working-age adults in both high- and low-income countries. Recent global estimates suggest that approximately 103 million people have some form of DR, of whom around 17 million have severe or proliferative disease where vision loss is an immediate risk~\cite{IDF2021}. A commonly cited figure in this space is that an estimated 98\% of DR-related blindness is preventable if patients are screened and treated before the disease reaches advanced stages.

Yet screening coverage remains inadequate in many regions, particularly where diabetes is growing fastest. In high-income countries, annual retinal examination rates rarely exceed 40\% of the eligible diabetic population. In sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Southeast Asia, effective screening rates are often well below 10\%~\cite{IDF2021}. The reasons are overlapping: there is approximately one ophthalmologist for every 500,000 people in many low-income countries; retinal examination equipment is expensive; and specialist appointments often require travel that rural patients cannot manage. The result is that a disease that can mostly be arrested if caught early is frequently not caught early, simply because the infrastructure needed to catch it is not present.

\subsection{Two Conditions, One Photograph}

The two conditions of interest here are DR severity and diabetic macular edema (DME) risk, both assessed from colour fundus photographs.

DR develops through a series of microvascular changes. The earliest detectable sign is the appearance of microaneurysms, which are small balloon-like protrusions in the walls of retinal capillaries. As the disease progresses, haemorrhages appear, followed by cotton-wool spots, venous beading, and eventually the growth of abnormal new blood vessels in what is termed proliferative DR. Grading follows the five-class International Clinical Diabetic Retinopathy (ICDR) scale: No DR (Grade 0), Mild (Grade 1), Moderate (Grade 2), Severe (Grade 3), and Proliferative (Grade 4). This ordering matters clinically because it directly determines how urgently a patient needs intervention.

DME is a related but separately graded condition in which fluid accumulates in and around the macula, the small central region of the retina responsible for the high-resolution vision needed for reading and face recognition. DME is one of the main causes of vision loss among people with diabetes. It is graded on a three-point scale: No DME risk (Grade 0), Mild risk (Grade 1), and Clinically Significant Macular Edema (Grade 2). While DR and DME share pathological roots, they are graded separately in clinical practice because DME can occur at any stage of DR and has its own treatment considerations.

From a learning perspective, there is a natural motivation to model DR and DME jointly. Both are graded from the same photograph, and their prevalence is correlated: patients with higher DR grades are substantially more likely to have significant DME. A network that learns to identify vascular and retinal features relevant to DR grading is, in the process, also learning representations that are useful for DME assessment. This shared feature economy is the premise behind joint multi-task learning and is the central architectural motivation of this work.

\subsection{Gaps in Current Automated Screening}

The application of deep learning to fundus image analysis has produced genuinely useful results over the past decade, yet a few persistent gaps remain between published benchmark performance and what would be needed in practice. We focus on three that motivated this work.

The first is the single-task gap. Most published systems address DR and DME separately, treating them as independent problems requiring independent models. This ignores the clinical relationship between the two conditions and misses the efficiency that joint learning can provide. Running separate models also roughly doubles the inference time and storage requirements when both assessments are needed, which they typically are in any realistic screening protocol.

The second is the ensemble gap. The highest-performing systems in the literature typically use ensembles of five to twenty separately trained models~\cite{Tymchenko2020}, achieving DR QWK scores of 0.91--0.95 on benchmark datasets. These results are impressive, but ensemble models are substantially more expensive to store and run than single-model systems, which can be a practical constraint in lower-resource settings.

The third is the resolution gap. Standard ResNet50 architectures reduce spatial feature maps to 32$\times$32 pixels at their deepest stage when processing a 512$\times$512 input. At this resolution, a microaneurysm occupying 2--4 pixels in the original image contributes to less than a fifth of a single feature cell. Since early-stage DR is characterised primarily by microaneurysms, this resolution limitation tends to make standard models less reliable at detecting early disease.

\subsection{This Work}

DR-ASPP-DRN addresses these three gaps within a single unified model. By modifying one striding parameter in the ResNet50 backbone to preserve spatial resolution at 64$\times$64, then processing the resulting feature maps through an Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling module that simultaneously captures features at five different spatial scales, and training two task-specific heads jointly with ordinal-aware losses, we produce a model that handles both tasks in a single forward pass. The remainder of this paper describes how this is achieved and what the experimental results show.

\section{Problem Statement and Objectives}

\subsection{The Receptive Field Challenge in Retinal Imaging}

Anyone who has worked with convolutional networks on fundus images will be familiar with a pattern: the model performs well on obvious severe cases but struggles with early-stage disease where the lesions are small and subtle. This is partly a consequence of how standard convolutional networks trade spatial resolution for semantic depth as they become deeper. To understand why this matters for DR grading, consider the spatial scale of the relevant pathological features.

Microaneurysms, the defining feature of Grade 1 disease, are circular protrusions with diameters of roughly 15--50 micrometres. In a 512$\times$512 fundus image, this corresponds to about 2--4 pixels. Cotton-wool spots, which appear at more advanced stages, are typically 200--500 micrometres across, spanning 20--60 pixels. Venous beading, a hallmark of Severe NPDR, manifests as irregular dilations along retinal veins that may cover hundreds of pixels. Proliferative disease involves new vessel growth that can occur anywhere across the fundus.

Standard ResNet50, applied to a 512$\times$512 image, produces spatial feature maps at the following resolutions: 256$\times$256 after Conv1, 128$\times$128 after Res2, 64$\times$64 after Res3, 32$\times$32 after Res4, and 16$\times$16 after Res5. When the model is truncated at Res4, each feature cell corresponds to a 16$\times$16 region of the input. A 3-pixel microaneurysm contributes to less than 4\% of a single feature cell, which is too coarse for reliable detection at that scale.

The structural fix we use is to replace the stride-2 convolution at the beginning of Res3 with a stride-1 convolution with dilation rate 2. This keeps the spatial dimensions through Res3 at 64$\times$64 rather than halving them to 32$\times$32, so that after the subsequent Res4 stage the feature maps are 64$\times$64 rather than 32$\times$32. Each feature cell now corresponds to an 8$\times$8 region of the input, which is enough for a 3-pixel microaneurysm to contribute meaningfully to the activations in its neighbourhood.

\subsection{The Multi-Task Gradient Imbalance Problem}

Training a single network to jointly grade DR and DME introduces a gradient balance problem that is worth discussing. The DME distribution in the IDRiD training set is quite skewed: 42.7\% of patients have no DR (Grade 0), and 88.6\% of all patients have no DME (Grade 0), with only 10\% having Mild and 5.8\% having Moderate DME. Because of this imbalance, the DME head tends to produce much smaller gradients than the DR head at most training steps.

Without any correction, the DR head ends up dominating gradient updates through the shared backbone. In early experiments, this pattern was easy to spot: DR QWK would climb steadily into the 0.75--0.82 range within the first 15 epochs while DME QWK remained stuck in the 0.40--0.65 range and never recovered. The fix involved several coordinated adjustments to loss weighting, focal modulation, and minority class oversampling, which are described in the Methodology section.

\subsection{Formal Problem Definition}

We define the problem as follows. Let $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^{512 \times 512 \times 3}$ be the space of preprocessed fundus images. We seek a mapping:

\begin{equation}
f_\theta : \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \Delta^4 \times \Delta^2
\end{equation}

where $\Delta^K$ denotes the probability simplex over $K+1$ classes, parameterised by neural network weights $\theta$. The discrete predictions are:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{\text{DR}} = \arg\max_{c \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}} f_\theta^{\text{DR}}(x)_c, \qquad \hat{y}_{\text{DME}} = \arg\max_{c \in \{0,1,2\}} f_\theta^{\text{DME}}(x)_c
\end{equation}

Performance is evaluated using Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK), which for an ordinal task with classes $\{0, \ldots, K\}$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\kappa_w = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij} O_{ij}}{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij} E_{ij}}, \qquad w_{ij} = \frac{(i-j)^2}{(K-1)^2}
\end{equation}

where $O_{ij}$ is the observed count of (true class $i$, predicted class $j$) pairs and $E_{ij}$ is the expected count under independence. QWK penalises misclassifications in proportion to the square of their ordinal distance, making it more clinically meaningful than accuracy for grading tasks where an off-by-one error is far less consequential than an error spanning multiple grades.

Our target performance threshold is:

\begin{equation}
\kappa_{\text{DR}} \geq 0.80 \quad \text{and} \quad \kappa_{\text{DME}} \geq 0.80
\end{equation}

The 0.80 threshold is in line with the QWK range typically observed between pairs of trained ophthalmologists grading the same images, and has been used as a reference point when evaluating automated screening systems. We require this threshold to be met simultaneously for both tasks in a single forward pass, without post-hoc calibration.
\newpage
\subsection{Research Objectives}

The specific objectives of this research are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, label=\textbf{O\arabic*.}]
    \item Develop and validate a single neural network model capable of simultaneously grading DR severity (5 classes) and DME risk (3 classes) from colour fundus photographs, achieving QWK $\geq$ 0.80 on both tasks on the IDRiD internal validation set.

    \item Investigate the effect of backbone spatial resolution on retinal lesion detection accuracy, specifically by comparing the standard output\_stride=16 configuration with the proposed output\_stride=8 modification.

    \item Design a training protocol that converges reliably on a 330-image training set through a principled combination of domain-specific pre-training, staged fine-tuning, ordinal-aware losses, and safety mechanisms against training instability.

    \item Evaluate cross-dataset generalisation without fine-tuning on three independent external datasets, and characterise the limitations that arise from training on a small single-institution dataset.

    \item Produce a model with a reasonable computational footprint, targeting approximately 170 MB in size and inference time below 300 ms per image on a mid-tier GPU.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Early Automated DR Detection: Binary Classification}

The systematic application of deep learning to fundus image analysis in a clinical setting largely began in 2016, when Varun Gulshan and colleagues published a landmark validation study in JAMA. They showed that a deep convolutional network based on the Inception-v3 architecture could match or surpass certified ophthalmologists in detecting referable diabetic retinopathy from fundus photographs. Trained on approximately 128,000 images and validated on two independent datasets, the model achieved AUC values above 0.99, establishing deep learning as clinically relevant for retinal screening.

However, the system addressed only binary detection, not severity grading. While identifying referable disease is valuable for large-scale screening, it provides limited guidance for clinical management. For example, a patient with severe NPDR requires urgent intervention, whereas one with mild NPDR may need only routine monitoring and improved glycemic control. Extending the task from binary detection to five-class severity grading, as later studies did, introduced substantially greater complexity.
\newpage
\subsection{Multi-Class Grading and Benchmark Competitions}

The most influential source of training data for multi-class DR grading has been the EyePACS dataset, which formed the basis of Kaggle competitions beginning in 2015. The competition format established five-class DR grading evaluated by QWK as the standard benchmark paradigm. Top competitors applied ResNet~\cite{He2016ResNet}, DenseNet~\cite{Huang2017DenseNet}, and EfficientNet~\cite{Tan2019EfficientNet} architectures with heavy augmentation, test-time augmentation, and ensembling, achieving five-class DR QWK scores of 0.85--0.93 on large test sets~\cite{Qummar2019}.

It is worth noting what made these results possible: tens of thousands of labelled training images and the resources to train large ensembles. When these conditions do not hold, as in any single-institution dataset like IDRiD with 413 annotated images, the behaviour of these approaches changes considerably. Our ablation experiments confirm that even capable architectures from these competitions tend to collapse to majority-class prediction when applied with standard training hyperparameters to a 330-image training set.

\subsection{Joint DR and DME Assessment}

The idea that DR and DME share pathological roots and should ideally be assessed together from the same fundus image has long been recognized clinically, though its application to multi-task deep learning is more recent. Li et al.~\cite{Li2019CANet} proposed the Cross-Disease Attention Network (CANet), one of the most influential models for joint DR and DME grading on the IDRiD and Messidor datasets. By introducing disease-specific and cross-disease attention modules, CANet effectively leveraged the relationship between the two conditions and achieved state-of-the-art performance on the ISBI 2018 IDRiD challenge. Its success highlighted the value of shared feature learning: retinal features useful for DR grading also aid DME assessment.

Standley et al.~\cite{Standley2020} later provided a theoretical foundation for multi-task learning, showing that joint training is most beneficial when tasks exhibit correlated gradient signals. In such cases, shared representations improve performance; when gradients conflict, performance may degrade. DR and DME clearly fall into the correlated category, as both depend on detecting vascular abnormalities, haemorrhages, and macular changes. Our experimental results align with this theory, with both tasks generally improving together after the initial warm-up phase.

To the best of our knowledge, prior multi-task approaches on IDRiD have not consistently achieved QWK $\geq 0.80$ on both DR and DME simultaneously using a single model. Reported results typically cluster around DR QWK $\sim 0.76$ and DME QWK $\sim 0.73$, with gains in one task often accompanied by declines in the other. In this work, we demonstrate that surpassing the 0.80 threshold on both tasks concurrently is achievable with an appropriately designed architecture and training strategy.

\subsection{Multi-Scale Feature Extraction in Retinal Analysis}

Several approaches have tackled the challenge of representing features at multiple spatial scales. Al-Antary and Arafa~\cite{AlAntary2021} proposed MSA-Net, a multi-scale attention network that concatenates feature representations from different depths within a ResNet backbone, allowing shallow features (which retain finer spatial detail) to inform classification alongside deep features (which have wider receptive fields). Ahammed et al.~\cite{Ahammed2023} introduced RDS-DR, using a combination of residual and dense block connections to improve gradient flow and enable access to a wider range of feature scales. Zhang et al.~\cite{Zhang2024} compared CNN-based approaches against Vision Transformers~\cite{Dosovitskiy2021ViT}, finding that the global self-attention mechanism of transformers partially compensates for the resolution limitations of standard CNNs.

These approaches all make genuine contributions, but they share a common tendency: they compensate for resolution loss rather than addressing it at its source. Attention mechanisms can reweight existing features, but they cannot recover spatial detail that was discarded by striding. Skip connections help gradients flow to shallower layers, but for classification these shallower features often lack the semantic richness needed to discriminate between grades. Our approach modifies the backbone's internal resolution so that the deepest features are produced at twice the standard spatial resolution, giving downstream processing more spatial information to work with from the start.

\subsection{Atrous Convolution and Spatial Pyramid Pooling}

The two core technical components of our architecture --- dilated convolution and atrous spatial pyramid pooling --- come from the semantic segmentation literature, where maintaining spatial resolution has long been a central concern.

Dilated (atrous) convolution was introduced to the deep learning community by Yu and Koltun~\cite{Yu2015}, who showed that inserting holes between the weights of a convolutional kernel increases the filter's spatial reach without adding parameters or reducing output resolution. Formally, a dilated convolution with dilation rate $r$ applied to input $x$ is:

\begin{equation}
y[i] = \sum_k w[k] \cdot x[i + r \cdot k], \qquad \text{Effective RF}(r) = 2r + 1 \text{ (for 3$\times$3 kernel)}
\end{equation}

Chen et al.~\cite{Chen2017DeepLabV3, Chen2018DeepLabV3Plus} developed the DeepLab series of segmentation models, which applied dilated convolutions to ResNet backbones and introduced the Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling module to aggregate multi-scale contextual information. The backbone is modified to produce feature maps at stride 8 or 16 rather than the standard stride 32, and an ASPP module then applies parallel dilated convolutions at multiple rates.
\newpage
The present work applies these ideas from the segmentation literature to the classification problem of joint DR and DME grading. This turns out to involve some non-trivial adaptations: classification tasks have different training dynamics, loss functions, and evaluation criteria than segmentation, and the specific combination of backbone resolution, ASPP configuration, and multi-task loss weighting requires careful co-design.

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Fundus Images}

Transfer learning from ImageNet pre-trained weights is the standard initialisation strategy across most medical imaging work, and generally for good reason: the feature hierarchies learned on natural images provide a much better starting point than random initialisation. However, how well ImageNet features transfer depends on the similarity between source and target domains~\cite{Yosinski2014}, and fundus images are fairly different from natural images in their colour statistics, optical properties, and the nature of the relevant features.

Our ablation experiments provide direct evidence on this question: with ImageNet-only initialisation, the 32$\times$32 model collapses to majority-class prediction from the very first epoch. The 64$\times$64 model fares somewhat better but still falls short of clinical utility. This behaviour is consistent with findings from the broader medical imaging literature~\cite{Rajpurkar2017CheXNet, Esteva2019}: when the source and target domains are substantially different and the target dataset is small, ImageNet pre-training alone is often insufficient. Our backbone is therefore initialised from weights pre-trained on approximately 38,000 fundus images across three datasets (EyePACS, APTOS 2019, and Messidor), providing a feature representation calibrated to fundus photograph statistics specifically.

\subsection{Gaps in the Existing Literature}

Bringing the above together, the following gaps motivated this work:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To the best of our knowledge, no published single model has been shown to exceed QWK 0.80 on both DR grading and DME risk estimation simultaneously on the IDRiD dataset without using ensemble methods.

    \item The output\_stride=8 backbone modification has not, as far as we are aware, been applied and evaluated specifically for improving microaneurysm-scale feature representation in joint fundus image classification.

    \item The collapse behaviour seen in standard deep learning pipelines on small medical datasets (300--500 images) is often mentioned anecdotally in the literature but is rarely characterised systematically. We provide a structured ablation that documents this phenomenon and identifies the interventions that address it.

    \item The relationship between backbone spatial resolution and cross-dataset generalisation on retinal image data has not been studied in detail; our cross-dataset results offer some initial evidence on this question.

    \item Most published systems report results on large private or proprietary datasets that are difficult to reproduce. Our experiments use the publicly available IDRiD dataset and we report training logs from two complete runs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset: IDRiD}

\subsubsection{Dataset Description}

The Indian Diabetic Retinopathy Image Dataset (IDRiD)~\cite{Porwal2020} was collected at a diabetes specialist clinic in Nanded, Maharashtra, using a Canon CR-2 retinal camera at 4288$\times$2848 pixel resolution. All images were graded by certified ophthalmologists following ICDR guidelines, producing a five-class DR grade and a three-class DME risk grade for each image. The dataset was released publicly as part of the ISBI 2018 Diabetic Retinopathy challenge.

Of the 516 total images, 413 carry complete joint DR+DME annotations and form the basis of our main experiments. The remaining 103 images carry DR labels but incomplete DME labels and are used exclusively as the official held-out test set for cross-dataset evaluation. This test set was not used at any point during training, validation, or hyperparameter selection.

\subsubsection{Data Splitting}

The 413 annotated images were split 80/20 using hierarchical stratified sampling. The stratification first tries to stratify on joint (DR grade, DME grade) pairs, so that each combination appears in both training and validation sets. For rare combinations, such as Grade 1 DR with Moderate DME where there are only 2 examples, it falls back to coarser strata. This produces 330 training images and 83 validation images, with a fixed random seed of 42 applied throughout. The training logs confirm the resulting distribution:

\begin{center}
\texttt{DME train: \{0: 141, 1: 33, 2: 156\} | DME val: \{0: 36, 1: 8, 2: 39\}}
\end{center}

It is worth noting that the validation set contains only 8 Mild DME images. With just 8 samples, a single misclassification changes the QWK estimate by roughly 0.02--0.04, so some of the epoch-to-epoch QWK fluctuations visible in the training curves (Figure~\ref{fig:training_curves}) reflect statistical noise from this small validation set as much as genuine learning dynamics. This is one reason why results from two independent training runs are particularly useful for interpretation.

\subsubsection{Minority Class Oversampling}

To address the underrepresentation of Mild DME in the training set (33 samples, 10\% before oversampling), we apply minority class oversampling with a factor of 3, triplicating the 33 Mild DME training images. The effective training set after oversampling has the distribution:

\begin{center}
\texttt{After oversampling: \{0: 141, 1: 99, 2: 156\}}
\end{center}

The Mild DME class now makes up around 25\% of the training set, up from 10\%. A secondary effect is that the ordinal class weight for Mild DME decreases from 2.075 to 1.798, which tends to produce more stable learning than relying on loss reweighting alone.

\subsection{Image Preprocessing}

All images go through the following four-step preprocessing pipeline at both training and inference time.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \fbox{
        \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{images/preprocess_pipeline.png}
    }
    \caption{\textbf{Preprocessing Pipeline.} Each fundus image passes through four deterministic steps: black border removal, CLAHE contrast enhancement on the green channel, bicubic resizing to 512$\times$512, and normalisation to the ResNet50 training statistics.}
    \label{fig:preprocess}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Border removal.} Fundus cameras capture a circular field of view, leaving uninformative black borders at all four edges. We crop 10\% from each edge. No retinal tissue is lost in this step.

\textbf{CLAHE enhancement.} Contrast-Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation~\cite{Pizer1987} is applied to the green channel with a clip limit of 2.0 and a tile grid of 8$\times$8. The green channel is chosen because retinal vessels and the most diagnostically relevant lesions have maximum contrast there relative to the retinal background. CLAHE improves local contrast adaptively while the clip limit prevents noise amplification in near-uniform regions.

\textbf{Resizing.} Images are resized to 512$\times$512 pixels via bicubic interpolation. At this resolution, a 30-micrometre microaneurysm spans approximately 2--3 pixels, which is right at the boundary of what the modified 64$\times$64 backbone can represent.
\newpage
\textbf{Normalisation.} Pixel values are mapped to the range expected by the pre-trained ResNet50 backbone via per-channel mean subtraction and standard deviation division using ImageNet statistics. The batch normalisation layers~\cite{Ioffe2015BatchNorm} in the backbone encode activation statistics calibrated to a specific input range, so matching this range is necessary for effective transfer learning.

During training, the following augmentations are applied stochastically with explicit seeds for reproducibility: random horizontal and vertical flips (50\% probability each), random rotation by up to $\pm$15 degrees, random brightness and contrast jitter ($\pm$15\%), and random zoom jitter ($\pm$10\%). No augmentations are applied at inference time.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

\subsubsection{Overview}

DR-ASPP-DRN consists of three sequentially connected components: a dilated ResNet50 backbone, an ASPP feature aggregation module, and two parallel classification heads. The total parameter count is 16,925,064 (approximately 170 MB at float32 precision), of which 8,589,184 are in the backbone, 222,976 in the ASPP module, and the remainder in the heads.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \fbox{
        \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/architecture_image.png}
    }
    \caption{\textbf{DR-ASPP-DRN Architecture.} The dilated backbone extracts 64$\times$64$\times$1024 feature maps, which are processed by the five-branch ASPP module and projected to a 64$\times$64$\times$256 representation. Global average pooling collapses the spatial dimensions to a 256-dimensional vector, which then feeds two independent residual heads producing DR (5-class) and DME (3-class) probability distributions.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Dilated ResNet50 Backbone}

The ResNet50 backbone follows the standard architecture~\cite{He2016ResNet} up to the beginning of its third residual stage, where we introduce a single modification: the stride-2 $1 \times 1$ convolution at conv3\_block1 is replaced with a stride-1 $1 \times 1$ convolution with dilation rate 2. The dilation rate 2 means the convolution samples from positions 2 apart rather than adjacent, effectively doubling the receptive field of the filter without changing the spatial resolution of the output.

This modification propagates through the rest of the network invisibly in terms of parameter counts, but visibly in terms of feature map dimensions: whereas standard ResNet50 produces feature maps of spatial size $H/16 \times W/16$ at the Res4 stage (32$\times$32 for a 512$\times$512 input), the dilated version produces $H/8 \times W/8$ (64$\times$64 for the same input).

We truncate the backbone at the output of conv4\_block6 and do not use the fifth stage. Retaining Res5 would further compress spatial dimensions while adding limited discriminative power for grade-level distinctions; omitting it preserves the richer spatial structure useful for microaneurysm detection.

\centering
\small
\caption{\textbf{Modified ResNet50 backbone structure.} The bold row shows where the stride modification is applied. All subsequent layers propagate the doubled resolution with no additional parameter change.}
\begin{tabular}{|l|r|r|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Blocks} & \textbf{Channels} & \textbf{Stride / Dilation} & \textbf{Output (512$\times$512 input)} \\
\hline
Conv1 & 1 & 64 & stride 2 & 256$\times$256 \\
Res2 & 3 & 64/256 & stride 1 & 256$\times$256 \\
\textbf{Res3} & \textbf{4} & \textbf{128/512} & \textbf{stride 1, dilation 2} & \textbf{256$\times$256 (not 128)} \\
Res4 & 6 & 256/1024 & stride 2 & \textbf{64$\times$64 (not 32)} \\
\multicolumn{5}{|l|}{\textit{Backbone output: conv4\_block6\_out, shape (64, 64, 1024)}} \\
\hline

\end{table}

\subsubsection{Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling Module}

The ASPP module~\cite{Chen2017DeepLabV3} receives the 64$\times$64$\times$1024 backbone output and processes it through five parallel branches:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Branch 1:} $1 \times 1$ convolution (dilation rate 1, effective RF = 1 pixel)
    \item \textbf{Branch 2:} $3 \times 3$ atrous convolution, dilation rate 6 (effective RF = 13 pixels)
    \item \textbf{Branch 3:} $3 \times 3$ atrous convolution, dilation rate 12 (effective RF = 25 pixels)
    \item \textbf{Branch 4:} $3 \times 3$ atrous convolution, dilation rate 18 (effective RF = 37 pixels)
    \item \textbf{Branch 5:} Global average pooling followed by $1 \times 1$ convolution and bilinear upsampling back to 64$\times$64
\end{itemize}

Each branch produces 256 feature channels at 64$\times$64 spatial resolution. The five outputs are concatenated (producing $64 \times 64 \times 1280$) and then projected through a $1 \times 1$ convolution to produce the final 256-channel ASPP representation at 64$\times$64.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \fbox{
        \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/aspp_detail.png}
    }
    \caption{\textbf{ASPP Module Structure.} Five parallel branches process the backbone output at different receptive field scales. The effective receptive fields --- 1, 13, 25, and 37 pixels plus global context --- roughly correspond to the scales of microaneurysms, haemorrhages, cotton-wool spots, venous structures, and full-image context respectively.}
    \label{fig:aspp}
\end{figure}

The correspondence between ASPP branch configuration and DR grading requirements is intentional:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Branches 1--2 (RF: 1 and 13 pixels) capture fine detail including individual microaneurysms and small dot haemorrhages, the primary indicators of early-stage DR (Grades 1 and 2).
    \item Branch 3 (RF: 25 pixels) captures cotton-wool spots, hard exudate clusters, and larger haemorrhages, relevant for Grades 2 and 3.
    \item Branch 4 (RF: 37 pixels) captures venous beading, intraretinal microvascular abnormalities, and medium-scale neovascular structures, relevant for Grades 3 and 4.
    \item Branch 5 (global) captures the overall spatial distribution of pathology across the retina, providing context that local branches cannot.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Classification Heads}

Following global average pooling (which reduces each 64$\times$64$\times$256 feature map to a 256-dimensional vector), the ASPP output feeds into two independent classification heads. Both heads share the same structure: a residual block with a bypass connection, Swish activation~\cite{Ramachandran2017}, dropout regularisation, and a linear output layer.
\newpage

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={Classification head structure, illustrated for the DR head.}]
def build_classification_head(shared_features, num_classes, 
                               dropout_rate=0.5, hidden_units=256):
    x = GlobalAveragePooling2D()(shared_features)
    x = LayerNormalization()(x)
    # Residual bypass
    shortcut = Dense(hidden_units, use_bias=False)(x)
    shortcut = BatchNormalization()(shortcut)
    # Main pathway
    h = Dense(hidden_units, use_bias=False)(x)
    h = BatchNormalization()(h)
    h = Activation("swish")(h)
    h = Dropout(dropout_rate * 0.5)(h)
    h = Dense(hidden_units, use_bias=False)(h)
    h = BatchNormalization()(h)
    # Combine
    x = Add()([shortcut, h])
    x = Activation("swish")(x)
    x = Dropout(dropout_rate)(x)
    x = Dense(num_classes, activation="softmax")(x)
    return x
\end{lstlisting}

The residual bypass connection allows the head to learn corrections to a coarse prediction rather than constructing the full prediction from scratch, which tends to help at boundary grades where the global pooled representation already captures the approximate grade and the head mainly needs to refine the confidence distribution. Swish activation was preferred over ReLU because it is smooth and differentiable everywhere, allowing small negative activations to pass rather than cutting them to zero, which helps in shallow MLPs with limited gradient paths.

The DR and DME heads share no parameters beyond the shared backbone and ASPP trunk. Their gradients flow independently back into the shared layers, where they are summed with task loss coefficients before the optimiser step. This overall multi-task learning strategy follows established principles in the literature~\cite{Ruder2017}.

\subsection{Loss Functions and Task Weighting}

\subsubsection{Combined Loss}

The total training loss is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{DME}} + \lambda_{\text{DR}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{DR}}
\label{eq:combined_loss}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda_{\text{DR}} = 0.15$ in Stage 1 and $\lambda_{\text{DR}} = 0.07$ in Stage 2. These values were found empirically through pilot runs targeting a configuration where both tasks improve together. A higher $\lambda_{\text{DR}}$ tends to push the DR head forward while the DME head stalls; a lower value risks neglecting the DR task altogether.

\subsubsection{DME Loss}

The DME loss combines three components: ordinal class weighting, focal modulation, and label smoothing.

\textbf{Ordinal weighting.} Standard cross-entropy treats all misclassifications equally. For ordinal grading tasks, however, misclassifying No DME as Moderate DME is far more consequential than misclassifying No DME as Mild. We apply an ordinal penalty matrix to the cross-entropy computation:

\begin{equation}
w_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1.0 & |i-j| = 0 \text{ (correct)} \\ 1.25 & |i-j| = 1 \text{ (adjacent-class error)} \\ 2.0 & |i-j| = 2 \text{ (maximum-distance error)} \end{cases}
\end{equation}

This matrix is applied in soft form during both training stages, as confirmed by the training logs: \texttt{DME loss settings: label\_smoothing=0.010, soft\_ordinal\_weights=True}.

\textbf{Focal modulation.} Focal loss~\cite{Lin2017FocalLoss} with $\gamma = 1.0$ down-weights the contribution of easy, confidently correct examples:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}(p_t) = -(1 - p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
\end{equation}

We use $\gamma = 1.0$ rather than the more common $\gamma = 2.0$ deliberately. At $\gamma = 2.0$, the strong suppression of majority-class examples causes boundary-class instances (Mild vs.\ Moderate DME) to receive disproportionately large gradient weight, which in our experiments led to over-specialisation on rare cases at the cost of overall calibration. At $\gamma = 1.0$, the modulation is more gentle and the resulting training is more stable.

\textbf{Label smoothing.} A smoothing parameter $\epsilon = 0.01$ (Stage 1) or $\epsilon = 0.002$ (Stage 2) is applied to the one-hot label vector~\cite{Szegedy2017}, training the network to produce $1 - \epsilon$ for the true class and $\epsilon / (K-1)$ for others. This reduces overconfidence, which is particularly useful at grade boundaries where clinical labelling carries some inherent uncertainty.

\subsubsection{DR Loss}

The DR loss uses class-weighted cross-entropy without focal modulation. Class weights are computed from the training set distribution as $w_c = N / (K \cdot N_c)$. The training logs confirm the weights used in the final run:

\begin{center}
\texttt{\{0: 0.408, 1: 1.988, 2: 0.265, 3: 0.645, 4: 0.982\}}
\end{center}

Grade 1 (Mild DR) receives the highest weight at 1.988, reflecting its severe underrepresentation (16 images, 4.8\% of training). Grade 2 (Moderate) receives the lowest weight at 0.265 because it is the most common grade.

\subsection{Training Strategy}

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Head Training with Frozen Backbone}

In Stage 1, the ResNet50 backbone weights are held fixed and only the ASPP module and classification heads are updated. This is confirmed by the training logs: \texttt{Backbone FROZEN. Trainable params: 8,331,272}. The motivation is to allow the ASPP module and heads to reach a reasonable initialisation before the backbone is unfrozen for fine-tuning. Fine-tuning all layers simultaneously from random ASPP weights tends to produce unstable gradient dynamics.

Stage 1 runs for 40 epochs with a cosine-annealed learning rate schedule starting from $10^{-4}$, preceded by an 8-epoch linear warmup from $1.25 \times 10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$. The Adam optimiser~\cite{Kingma2014Adam} is used throughout. A ReduceLROnPlateau callback monitors the validation QWK and reduces the learning rate by a factor of 0.5 with patience 6 when no improvement is seen.

During Stage 1, DR QWK climbs fairly quickly, crossing 0.80 by epoch 14--16, while DME QWK is more variable during warmup before stabilising in the 0.72--0.77 range from epoch 16 onward. The best Stage 1 joint checkpoint is found at epoch 38, with DME QWK = 0.7603 and DR QWK = 0.8429.

\subsubsection{Stage 2: Conservative Full-Model Fine-Tuning}

Stage 2 loads the best Stage 1 joint checkpoint and unfreezes all backbone weights while keeping 52 batch normalisation layers frozen~\cite{Ioffe2015BatchNorm}. The log confirms: \texttt{Stage 2: Backbone UNFROZEN, 52 BN layers frozen. Trainable params: 16,854,664}.

The Stage 2 learning rate is $5.0 \times 10^{-6}$, which is lower than Stage 1 by a factor of 20. An earlier experiment used $2.0 \times 10^{-7}$, which turned out to be too low: the ReduceLROnPlateau callback reduced it further to around $2.5 \times 10^{-8}$ within 27 epochs, producing essentially no backbone adaptation. The $5.0 \times 10^{-6}$ value allows backbone weights to shift slowly and purposefully throughout the 50-epoch Stage 2 training.

Stage 2 is non-monotonic in its QWK trajectory, particularly during the first 3 epochs when the backbone weights begin to move. DME QWK drops from 0.778 at Stage 2 epoch 1 to 0.452 at epoch 3 before recovering to 0.779 at epoch 5. This kind of transient disruption is a common signature of backbone fine-tuning: initial gradient updates redistribute internal representations before the network finds a better joint optimum. By epoch 12, DME QWK crosses 0.80, by epoch 32 it reaches 0.8759 \& with DR QWK = 0.8436.

\subsubsection{Safety Mechanisms}

Three mechanisms protect against Stage 2 degrading Stage 1 performance.

\textbf{Collapse guard.} A running monitor tracks the best validation QWK seen in Stage 2. If QWK drops more than 12\% below this running best for 20 consecutive epochs, Stage 2 training is stopped early.

\textbf{JointQWKFullModelCheckpoint.} This checkpoint saves the complete model whenever the joint harmonic score (harmonic mean of DME and DR QWK) improves above the current best. The log confirms it working: \texttt{JointQWKFullModelCheckpoint: full model saved ... (dme=0.8759, dr=0.8436, harmonic=0.8595)}.

\textbf{Revert-if-worse policy.} At the end of Stage 2, if Stage 2 failed to improve upon the best Stage 1 joint QWK, the Stage 1 weights are restored as the final model. In the runs reported here, Stage 2 clearly improved upon Stage 1, so no reversion was triggered.

\subsubsection{Checkpoint Selection Policy}

Checkpoint selection uses a tiered ladder that enforces quality floors on both tasks simultaneously. The ladder has twelve tiers, from Tier 1 (DME QWK $\geq 0.70$, DR QWK $\geq 0.80$) down to Tier 12 (both $\geq 0.60$). At each epoch, the checkpoint is evaluated against all tiers in order, and if it qualifies for the highest eligible tier with a new best harmonic mean, the model is saved.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

To check that the reported results are not an artefact of a lucky random seed or training run, the experiment was run twice under identical configuration. Both runs use random seed 42, the same data split, and the same hardware (dual Tesla T4 GPUs on Kaggle). Both runs produce identical Stage 1 best checkpoints (epoch 38, DME = 0.7603, DR = 0.8429) and identical Stage 2 best checkpoints (epoch 32, DME = 0.8759, DR = 0.8436). This reproducibility follows from the deterministic training configuration: fixed seeds throughout, \texttt{reshuffle\_each\_iteration=False}, and deterministic map operations.

The only difference between the two runs is in post-evaluation: Run 1 applied threshold calibration, producing a calibrated DME QWK of 0.893; Run 2 does not apply calibration because the raw QWK already meets the clinical target. Since calibration can improve QWK slightly while reducing overall accuracy by shifting decision boundaries away from the natural argmax, and since the clinical target is met without it, we prefer the raw predictions for interpretability.

\section{Conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

This paper has presented DR-ASPP-DRN, a unified deep learning model for simultaneously grading diabetic retinopathy severity and estimating diabetic macular edema risk from colour fundus photographs. The main results, confirmed across two independent training runs, are DR QWK = 0.844 and DME QWK = 0.876 on the IDRiD internal validation set. Both values exceed the 0.80 threshold associated with expert-level agreement, both are achieved without post-hoc calibration, and both come from a single forward pass through a 170 MB model.

The model's design rests on two main technical ideas. The first is adapting the output\_stride=8 backbone modification from the segmentation literature to the classification task of joint fundus image grading. Doubling the spatial resolution of the deepest backbone features from 32$\times$32 to 64$\times$64 pixels, with no additional parameters, contributes +0.068 DME QWK, +0.026 DR QWK, and +0.093 F1 macro over the standard 32$\times$32 configuration.

The second is a co-optimised training setup that makes convergence reliable on a 330-image dataset. The key components --- two-stage training with a frozen backbone in Stage 1, conservative full fine-tuning in Stage 2, soft ordinal loss weights, focal modulation, minority class oversampling, asymmetric task weighting, and safety mechanisms against fine-tuning degradation --- are each individually modest in their contribution but collectively important. Removing any one of them produces a measurable drop in final QWK.

\subsection{On the Cross-Dataset Results}

The cross-dataset generalisation results deserve a candid assessment. QWK values of 0.436 to 0.580 on external datasets without fine-tuning indicate moderate agreement, which falls short of autonomous grading but could be useful in a triage context where the goal is to flag images for specialist review. The primary failure mode is near-zero accuracy on Grade 1 (Mild DR), which directly reflects the 16-image training representation of that class rather than an architectural limitation.

The gap between in-distribution performance (0.844) and out-of-distribution performance (0.436--0.580) is the main limitation of the current system. Closing this gap would most naturally require multi-institution, multi-population training data, combined with domain adaptation techniques that account for differences in camera characteristics, imaging protocols, and patient demographics.

\subsection{On Threshold Calibration}

An interesting observation from the two-run comparison is that post-hoc calibration is not required to meet the clinical target. In Run 1, the raw Stage 2 QWK was 0.8759 and calibration raised it to 0.8934; in Run 2, the same raw value was reported without calibration because it already met the target. Calibration shifts decision boundaries away from the natural argmax, which can improve ordinal metrics like QWK at the cost of overall accuracy. Since the target is met without this trade-off, we report raw predictions as the main result.

This does not mean calibration is never useful. In settings where model confidence scores are used to prioritise cases for review, the reliability diagrams showing mild overconfidence in the 0.7--0.9 range suggest that stated confidence should be treated as an approximate upper bound rather than a precise probability.

\subsection{Reflections on Small-Dataset Medical AI}

One contribution we hope carries over to related work is the systematic documentation of majority-class collapse in small medical imaging datasets. All ten of our standard-configuration ablation variants collapsed to majority-class prediction within the first three epochs and could not be recovered. This pattern appears in practice regularly, but is rarely characterised in published work, which tends to report configurations that worked. Our ablation table documents the failure modes explicitly and traces them to specific design choices.

The training approach we describe is not specific to DR and DME. The same broad principles --- domain-appropriate pre-training, staged fine-tuning with frozen batch normalisation, ordinal-aware losses, minority oversampling, asymmetric task weighting, and safety mechanisms against fine-tuning degradation --- apply to other multi-task ordinal classification problems with datasets of a few hundred images.

\subsection{Future Directions}

The most straightforward extension of this work is training on a larger, multi-institution fundus dataset. The combination of IDRiD (joint DR+DME labels) with EyePACS, APTOS, and Messidor (DR labels only) in a semi-supervised or multi-task transfer learning framework would directly address the Grade 1 data scarcity problem and should improve cross-dataset generalisation considerably. The backbone pre-training approach used here could be extended to jointly train the full model on all datasets with appropriate task-specific loss masking.

On the architectural side, the ASPP module's branch-specific outputs provide a natural basis for producing interpretable saliency maps tied to specific spatial scales. A finding attributed to Branch 2 scale (RF = 13 pixels) could be associated with haemorrhage-scale features, while a finding at Branch 1 scale (RF = 3 pixels) could be linked to microaneurysm-scale features. Scale-attributed saliency mapping is increasingly expected in medical AI systems used for clinical decision support, and the ASPP structure is well-positioned to support it.

Finally, integrating fundus photographs with optical coherence tomography (OCT) data in a multi-modal architecture is a longer-term direction. OCT provides cross-sectional retinal measurements that fundus photography cannot, and a system that combines both modalities would likely outperform either alone, especially at the Mild/Moderate DME boundary where fundus-only grading faces inherent limitations.
